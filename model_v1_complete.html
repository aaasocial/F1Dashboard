F1 Tire Degradation Model from Public Telemetry

A five-module physics-informed model extending Castellano et al. (2021) to publicly available FastF1 data with Hertzian contact mechanics and thermal coupling.

Architecture overview

Module A — Kinematic front-end: XY position + speed → accelerations, path curvature, heading
Module B — Vertical loads: weight transfer + aero → $F_{z,i}$ per tire
Module C — Lateral/longitudinal forces: Castellano's proportional-to-load split → $F_{y,i}$, $F_{x,i}$
Module D — Hertzian contact + friction: contact patch geometry → $\bar{p}_i$ → $\mu_i(T, \bar{p}_i)$
Module E — Slip inversion + sliding power: brush model inversion → $\alpha_i, V_{sy,i}$ → $P_{slide,i}$
Module F — Thermal ODE: three-node lumped model → $T_{tread,i}(t)$ feedback
Module G — Cumulative energy + degradation: integrated $E_{tire,i}(t)$ → grip decay

Modules B and C follow Castellano's framework closely. Modules A, D, E, F, G are where this model diverges — using only public data, adding contact mechanics, and closing the thermal loop.

FastF1 telemetry │ V, X, Y, Z, RPM, throttle, brake, T_track, T_air ▼ [Module A: Kinematic front-end] │ a_lat, a_long, κ, ψ (heading), V_sx ▼ [Module B: Vertical loads per tire] ◄── gravity, weight transfer, aero │ F_z,FL F_z,FR F_z,RL F_z,RR ▼ [Module C: Force distribution (Castellano)] │ F_y,i F_x,i for each tire ▼ [Module D: Hertzian + friction] ◄── T_tread from Module F │ a_cp,i p̄_i μ_i(T, p̄) ▼ [Module E: Slip inversion & sliding power] │ α_i V_sy,i P_slide,i ▼ [Module F: Thermal ODE] ──── feeds T_tread back to Module D │ T_tread,i T_carc,i T_gas,i ▼ [Module G: Energy integral & degradation] E_cum,i → μ_0(t) (slow drift)

Module A — Kinematic front-end NEW

Castellano reads $A_x, A_y$ directly from an accelerometer. You must derive them from position and speed.

A.1 Path curvature from XY position

Fit a smoothing cubic spline through $(X(s), Y(s))$ parameterized by arc length $s$. Curvature:

NEW — replaces Castellano's accelerometer $$\kappa(s) = \dfrac{X'(s)\,Y''(s) - Y'(s)\,X''(s)}{\left(X'(s)^2 + Y'(s)^2\right)^{3/2}}$$

where primes denote $d/ds$. Because $s$ is arc length, $X'^2 + Y'^2 = 1$ by construction, so this simplifies to:

$$\kappa(s) = X'(s)\,Y''(s) - Y'(s)\,X''(s)$$

In practice, use a reference curvature map $\kappa_{ref}(s)$ computed once from the fastest clean lap, then look up $\kappa$ by track distance for every lap afterward. This eliminates lap-to-lap noise in the curvature signal.

A.2 Accelerations

NEW — Frenet-Serret decomposition $$a_{lat}(t) = V(t)^2 \cdot \kappa(s(t))$$
$$a_{long}(t) = \dfrac{dV}{dt}$$

Compute $dV/dt$ using a Savitzky-Golay filter on the speed channel to suppress high-frequency noise while preserving peaks.

A.3 Heading angle

The velocity vector direction (course angle):

$$\psi(t) = \arctan2\!\left(\dfrac{dY}{dt},\; \dfrac{dX}{dt}\right)$$

This is the direction of the velocity vector. The car's actual heading differs by the body sideslip angle $\beta$, which is small ($1{-}3°$) for F1 and ignored at this stage.

A.4 Longitudinal slip velocity

From RPM and gear ratios (assuming RWD):

$$V_{wheel,r}(t) = \dfrac{2\pi \cdot RPM(t)}{60 \cdot G_{ratio}(Gear) \cdot G_{final}} \cdot R_0$$
$$V_{sx,r}(t) = V_{wheel,r}(t) - V(t)$$

Positive during acceleration (wheel faster than ground), negative during braking. Gear ratios $G_{ratio}(1), \ldots, G_{ratio}(8)$ and final drive $G_{final}$ are FIA-regulated values. For front tires (undriven), $V_{sx,f} \approx 0$ except during braking — but since FastF1's Brake channel is only boolean, you treat $V_{sx,f}$ = 0 always and put the entire longitudinal sliding energy on the rear tires during acceleration and split it using brake bias during braking (as in Castellano §2.6).

Module B — Vertical loads per tire CASTELLANO

B.1 Static loads (Castellano Eqs. 1–2)

Castellano Eq. 1–2 $$SL_F = \dfrac{M_{tot} \cdot WD}{2}, \quad SL_R = \dfrac{M_{tot} \cdot (1-WD)}{2}$$

B.2 Longitudinal load transfer (Castellano Eq. 5)

Castellano Eq. 5 $$\Delta F_{z,long} = \dfrac{M_{tot} \cdot a_{long} \cdot H_{CG}}{WB}$$

B.3 Lateral load transfer SIMPLIFIED FROM CASTELLANO

Castellano's full lateral load transfer (Eq. 3–4) requires roll center heights, roll stiffnesses, and the roll angle $\theta$ measured from suspension displacement sensors. You have none of these. Use the simplified elastic model:

NEW — simplified, no roll angle sensor $$\Delta F_{z,lat,f} = \dfrac{M_{tot} \cdot a_{lat} \cdot H_{CG}}{T_f} \cdot \dfrac{K_{rf}}{K_{rf} + K_{rr}}$$
$$\Delta F_{z,lat,r} = \dfrac{M_{tot} \cdot a_{lat} \cdot H_{CG}}{T_r} \cdot \dfrac{K_{rr}}{K_{rf} + K_{rr}}$$

This captures the elastic (roll stiffness) component of load transfer but collapses the geometric component (roll centers). For F1 the simplification is acceptable because roll centers are low (20–50 mm vs. $H_{CG} \approx 280$ mm).

B.4 Aerodynamic downforce ADDED

Castellano's $F_{aero}$ is treated as a given per-corner quantity. For F1, compute it from speed and aero coefficients:

NEW — critical for F1 at high speed $$F_{z,aero}(V) = \tfrac{1}{2} \rho \, C_L A \cdot V^2$$

Split front/rear by aero balance $\xi$ (typically 0.45 for current F1):

$$F_{z,aero,f} = \xi \cdot F_{z,aero}, \quad F_{z,aero,r} = (1-\xi) \cdot F_{z,aero}$$

Per side (left/right), split equally. Aero balance can shift with ride height, but that data is not accessible — treat $\xi$ as constant.

B.5 Vertical load per tire (Castellano Eqs. 6–9)

Sign convention: $a_{lat} > 0$ for right turn (loads left side), $a_{long} > 0$ for acceleration (loads rear).

Castellano Eq. 6–9, with FL on outside of right turn $$F_{z,FL} = SL_F - \Delta F_{z,long} + \Delta F_{z,lat,f} + \tfrac{1}{2} F_{z,aero,f}$$
$$F_{z,FR} = SL_F - \Delta F_{z,long} - \Delta F_{z,lat,f} + \tfrac{1}{2} F_{z,aero,f}$$
$$F_{z,RL} = SL_R + \Delta F_{z,long} + \Delta F_{z,lat,r} + \tfrac{1}{2} F_{z,aero,r}$$
$$F_{z,RR} = SL_R + \Delta F_{z,long} - \Delta F_{z,lat,r} + \tfrac{1}{2} F_{z,aero,r}$$

Module C — Force distribution CASTELLANO

C.1 Total forces at CG (Castellano Eqs. 10–11)

Castellano Eq. 10–11 $$F_{y,G} = M_{tot} \cdot a_{lat}, \quad F_{x,G} = M_{tot} \cdot a_{long}$$

C.2 Lateral force per tire (Castellano Eqs. 12–19)

Proportional to vertical load share. Normalized load fractions:

Castellano Eqs. 16–19, adapted $$F_{z,i,\%} = \dfrac{F_{z,i}}{F_{z,FL} + F_{z,FR} + F_{z,RL} + F_{z,RR}}$$

Lateral force distribution:

Castellano Eqs. 12–15 $$F_{y,i} = F_{y,G} \cdot F_{z,i,\%} \quad \text{for } i \in \{FL, FR, RL, RR\}$$

C.3 Longitudinal force per tire (Castellano Eqs. 20–27)

Braking contribution, scaled by brake bias $BB$:

Castellano Eqs. 20–23 $$F_{x,i}^{brake} = \min\!\left(F_{x,G} \cdot F_{z,i,\%},\; 0\right) \cdot \begin{cases} BB & i \in \{FL, FR\} \\ 1-BB & i \in \{RL, RR\} \end{cases}$$

Power (acceleration) contribution — zero on fronts, rear-only:

Castellano Eqs. 24–27 $$F_{x,RL}^{power} = \max\!\left(F_{x,G}(F_{z,RL,\%} + F_{z,FL,\%}),\; 0\right)$$
$$F_{x,RR}^{power} = \max\!\left(F_{x,G}(F_{z,RR,\%} + F_{z,FR,\%}),\; 0\right)$$

Total: $F_{x,i} = F_{x,i}^{brake} + F_{x,i}^{power}$.

Module D — Hertzian contact + friction NEW

Castellano uses the team-provided Pacejka model as a black box. You replace this with a physics-based model built on Hertzian contact mechanics and Greenwood-Williamson adhesion theory.

D.1 Contact patch half-length

From Gim (1988), cited by Ozerem & Morrey (2019):

Gim (1988) $$a_{cp,i} = \sqrt{2 R_0 \delta_i}, \quad \delta_i = \dfrac{F_{z,i}}{K_{rad}}$$

Therefore $a_{cp,i} = \sqrt{2 R_0 F_{z,i} / K_{rad}}$, which scales as $F_{z,i}^{1/2}$. More heavily loaded tires have longer contact patches.

D.2 Mean Hertzian contact pressure

For a rectangular patch with tread half-width $b_{tread}$:

Hertzian, pneumatic tire approximation $$\bar{p}_i = \dfrac{F_{z,i}}{4 \, a_{cp,i} \, b_{tread}}$$

This pressure varies per tire as loads shift during cornering and braking.

D.3 Load-sensitive friction from Greenwood-Williamson

From Greenwood & Williamson (1966), the real contact area scales as:

Greenwood & Williamson (1966) $$A_{real} = \kappa_{GW} \cdot A_{app} \cdot \left(\dfrac{\bar{p}}{E^*}\right)^n$$

Since $F = \tau_s \cdot A_{real}$ and $F_z = \bar{p} \cdot A_{app}$, the friction coefficient $\mu = F/F_z$ becomes:

Derived from GW; absorbs unknown constants into $\mu_0$ $$\mu_i^{pressure}(\bar{p}_i) = \mu_0 \cdot \left(\dfrac{\bar{p}_0}{\bar{p}_i}\right)^{1-n}$$

where $\mu_0$ is the reference friction at reference pressure $\bar{p}_0$, and $n \approx 0.8$.

D.4 Temperature-dependent friction (Grosch-style bell curve)

The combined adhesion + hysteresis friction has a temperature optimum near the rubber's glass transition frequency response. Model with a Gaussian:

NEW — phenomenological, based on Grosch (1963) bell curve $$g(T_{tread}) = \exp\!\left(-\dfrac{(T_{tread} - T_{opt})^2}{2 \sigma_T^2}\right)$$

D.5 Complete friction model

MODEL CORE — combines D.3 and D.4 $$\mu_i(T_{tread,i}, \bar{p}_i) = \mu_0(t) \cdot \left(\dfrac{\bar{p}_0}{\bar{p}_i}\right)^{1-n} \cdot \exp\!\left(-\dfrac{(T_{tread,i} - T_{opt})^2}{2 \sigma_T^2}\right)$$

where $\mu_0(t)$ is the slowly-decaying reference friction (see Module G). At $t = 0$ on fresh tires, $\mu_0(0) = \mu_0^{fresh}$.

Module E — Slip inversion and sliding power NEW

Castellano inverts Pacejka to get slip angles from observed forces. You invert the brush model instead.

E.1 Brush model (Pacejka Ch. 3)

Normalized slip parameter:

Pacejka (2012), Ch. 3 $$\Theta_i = \dfrac{c_{py} \cdot a_{cp,i}^2}{3 \mu_i F_{z,i}} \cdot |\!\tan\alpha_i|$$

The total lateral force in the brush model:

Pacejka (2012), Ch. 3, Eq. 3.27 $$|F_{y,i}| = \mu_i F_{z,i} \left[1 - (1 - \Theta_i)^3\right], \quad \Theta_i \leq 1$$

E.2 Inversion

MODEL CORE — inverts brush model for $\Theta_i$ $$\Theta_i = 1 - \left(1 - \dfrac{|F_{y,i}|}{\mu_i F_{z,i}}\right)^{1/3}$$

Requires $|F_{y,i}| \leq \mu_i F_{z,i}$. If violated, clip $\Theta_i = 1$ (full sliding).

Recover the slip angle:

$$\alpha_i = \text{sgn}(F_{y,i}) \cdot \arctan\!\left(\dfrac{3 \mu_i F_{z,i}}{c_{py} \cdot a_{cp,i}^2} \cdot \Theta_i\right)$$

E.3 Lateral slip velocity

$$V_{sy,i} = V \cdot \sin(\alpha_i)$$

E.4 Sliding power

Following Kobayashi et al. (2019) and Castellano (2021):

Kobayashi (2019); Castellano (2021) Eqs. 40–47 $$P_{slide,i}(t) = |F_{y,i}| \cdot |V_{sy,i}| + |F_{x,i}| \cdot |V_{sx,i}|$$

E.5 Rolling resistance (background heat source)

Standard rolling resistance $$P_{rr,i}(t) = C_{rr} \cdot F_{z,i} \cdot V$$

with $C_{rr} \approx 0.012$ for racing slicks. Small compared to $P_{slide}$ in corners but provides baseline heating on straights.

E.6 Total power input per tire

$$P_{total,i}(t) = P_{slide,i}(t) + P_{rr,i}(t)$$

Module F — Thermal ODE NEW

Three-node lumped model following Sorniotti (2009) and Kenins et al. (2019).

F.1 Tread node

Sorniotti (2009); Kenins (2019) $$C_{tread} \dfrac{dT_{tread,i}}{dt} = \alpha_p P_{total,i} - h_{air}(V) A_{tread}(T_{tread,i} - T_{air}) - \dfrac{T_{tread,i} - T_{carc,i}}{R_{tc}}$$

F.2 Carcass node

$$C_{carc} \dfrac{dT_{carc,i}}{dt} = \dfrac{T_{tread,i} - T_{carc,i}}{R_{tc}} - h_{carc} A_{carc}(T_{carc,i} - T_{air}) - \dfrac{T_{carc,i} - T_{gas,i}}{R_{cg}}$$

F.3 Gas node

$$C_{gas} \dfrac{dT_{gas,i}}{dt} = \dfrac{T_{carc,i} - T_{gas,i}}{R_{cg}}$$

F.4 Heat partition coefficient

Fraction of sliding power entering the tire (remainder heats the road):

Farroni et al. (2015) $$\alpha_p = \dfrac{e_{rubber}}{e_{rubber} + e_{road}}, \quad e = \sqrt{k \rho c_p}$$

Approximately $\alpha_p \approx 0.5{-}0.6$ for rubber on asphalt.

F.5 Speed-dependent convection coefficient

Forced convection, Reynolds analogy $$h_{air}(V) = h_0 + h_1 \sqrt{V}$$

with $h_0 \approx 10$ W/m²K and $h_1 \approx 8$ W/m²K/(m/s)$^{1/2}$.

F.6 Initial conditions

At start of stint (after pit stop):

$$T_{tread,i}(0) = T_{carc,i}(0) = T_{gas,i}(0) = T_{track} + \Delta T_{blanket}$$

$\Delta T_{blanket} \approx 50{-}70°C$ for pre-heated tires per current FIA regulations.

F.7 Numerical integration

At 4 Hz telemetry ($\Delta t = 0.25$ s), forward Euler is stable for the tread and carcass time constants (both $> 5$ s):

$$T_{tread,i}(t + \Delta t) = T_{tread,i}(t) + \dot{T}_{tread,i}(t) \cdot \Delta t$$

Same for carcass and gas. For higher accuracy, upgrade to RK4.

Module G — Cumulative energy and degradation NEW

G.1 Cumulative tire energy

Todd et al. (2025); Castellano (2021) $$E_{tire,i}(t) = \int_0^t P_{total,i}(\tau) \, d\tau$$

Numerical: $E_{tire,i}(t+\Delta t) = E_{tire,i}(t) + P_{total,i}(t) \cdot \Delta t$.

G.2 Thermal degradation of reference friction

Sustained high temperatures cause permanent chemical degradation of the rubber — reversion of vulcanization, oxidation, and loss of network integrity. Model $\mu_0$ as a slowly decaying state:

NEW — Arrhenius-like thermal aging $$\dfrac{d\mu_0}{dt} = -\beta_{therm} \cdot \mu_0 \cdot \exp\!\left(\dfrac{T_{tread,i} - T_{ref}}{T_{act}}\right)$$

with $T_{act} \approx 20{-}30°C$ (every $T_{act}$ degrees doubles the degradation rate), and $\beta_{therm}$ calibrated from observed stint-end grip loss.

G.3 Mechanical wear

Rubber physically abraded from the tread:

NEW — energy-proportional wear $$\dfrac{d \, d_{tread}}{dt} = -k_{wear} \cdot P_{slide,i}$$

As $d_{tread}$ decreases, $R_{tc} = d_{tread}/(k_{rubber} A_{tread})$ decreases too — thinner tread heats and cools faster.

G.4 Lap-time penalty from grip loss

Corner speed scales as $V \propto \sqrt{\mu}$, so time through a corner scales as $1/\sqrt{\mu}$. A first-order expansion:

$$\Delta t_{lap}(t) \approx \dfrac{t_{lap}^{ref}}{2} \cdot \dfrac{\mu_0^{fresh} - \mu_0(t)}{\mu_0^{fresh}}$$

The feedback loop at each timestep

The coupling between Module D (friction) and Module F (thermal) creates a feedback loop that must be iterated:

  1. Read telemetry at time $t$: $V, X, Y, RPM, \ldots$
  2. Module A: compute $a_{lat}, a_{long}, V_{sx}$
  3. Module B: compute $F_{z,i}$ for each tire (uses $a_{lat}, a_{long}, V$)
  4. Module C: compute $F_{y,i}, F_{x,i}$ for each tire (uses $F_{z,i}$ from B)
  5. Module D: compute $a_{cp,i}, \bar{p}_i$, then $\mu_i$ using previous timestep's $T_{tread,i}$ and $\mu_0(t)$
  6. Module E: invert brush model to get $\Theta_i, \alpha_i, V_{sy,i}$. Compute $P_{slide,i}, P_{total,i}$.
  7. Module F: integrate thermal ODEs forward by $\Delta t$. Update $T_{tread,i}, T_{carc,i}, T_{gas,i}$.
  8. Module G: integrate $E_{tire,i}$ forward. Update $\mu_0$ via G.2. Update $d_{tread}$ via G.3.
  9. Repeat for $t + \Delta t$.
Why this converges without iteration: The friction at time $t$ uses the temperature at time $t$ (computed from energy accumulated through time $t$). This is causally correct — no iteration needed within a timestep. The feedback is in time, not within a step.
