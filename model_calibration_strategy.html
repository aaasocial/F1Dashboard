Model Calibration Strategy: Hyperparameters and Required Datasets

Separating fixed physics from learnable parameters, identifying the data we need, and sequencing the ML calibration pipeline.

Color key: FIXED = known from physics, FIA regs, or published specs. SEMI = estimable from data with moderate uncertainty. LEARNED = must be fitted from observed degradation data via ML/optimization.

Part II — Count of learnable parameters

Total LEARNED parameters per compound: ~18

  • Aero: 3 ($C_L A$, $C_D A$, $\xi$) — but these are car/track dependent, not compound dependent
  • Friction: 5 ($\mu_0^{fresh}$, $\bar{p}_0$, $n$, $T_{opt}$, $\sigma_T$) — per compound
  • Brush model: 1 ($c_{py}$) — per compound
  • Thermal: 8 ($C_{tread}, C_{carc}, C_{gas}, R_{tc}, R_{cg}, h_0, h_1, \alpha_p$) — compound-dependent, but many are shared
  • Degradation: 3 ($\beta_{therm}, T_{act}, k_{wear}$) — per compound
  • Setup: 1 ($K_{rf}/K_{rf}+K_{rr}$) — per team/track

For 5 Pirelli compounds (C1–C5), this gives 5 × (5+1+3) + 5×8 + 3 aero + 1 setup = ~95 parameters total. This is a lot, but many can be fixed from literature, many are shared across compounds, and the effective calibration dimension is much smaller.

Effective calibration dimension: The degradation-predicting parameters that matter most (and are least constrained by prior knowledge) are $\mu_0^{fresh}$, $n$, $T_{opt}$, $\sigma_T$, $\beta_{therm}$, and $C_{tread}/R_{tc}$ (which together set the thermal time constant). That's ~6 effective dimensions per compound. With 20+ races per season of training data, each providing ~50 degrading laps across 3–4 stints, calibration is well-posed.

Part III — Required datasets

Dataset 1 — Per-race FastF1 telemetry

Required: Every race from 2022 (start of ground-effect era) to present. For each race:

  • Per-driver car data: Speed, RPM, Gear, Throttle, Brake, DRS at ~4 Hz for every lap
  • Per-driver position data: X, Y, Z coordinates at ~4 Hz
  • Lap timing data: Lap times, sector times, compound, tire age, stint number, pit stops
  • Weather: Air temperature, track temperature, rainfall, wind, humidity, pressure

Approximate volume: 22 races/year × 20 drivers × 60 laps × 400 data points/lap × 10 channels ≈ 100M data points per season. Manageable on a laptop.

Dataset 2 — Track geometry reference

For each circuit, compute once:

  • Racing line curvature $\kappa(s)$ from aggregated fast laps
  • Elevation profile $Z(s)$ and vertical curvature $\kappa_z(s)$
  • Known corner radii, apex positions, sector boundaries
  • Estimated banking angles (where significant — Zandvoort T3, Istanbul T8 historically)

Dataset 3 — Pirelli compound specifications

Publicly available through Pirelli press releases and FIA technical documents:

  • Compound assignments per race (which of C1–C5 are the SOFT/MEDIUM/HARD at each GP)
  • Recommended operating temperature windows (published loosely)
  • Recommended starting pressures (when available)

Dataset 4 — Gear ratio inference

Not directly published. Infer from data:

  • Filter telemetry to steady-speed portions of straights (throttle = 100%, gear constant)
  • Compute ratio: gear-ratio × final-drive = $2\pi R_0 \cdot RPM / (60 \cdot V)$
  • Fit 8 ratios + final drive per team across their fastest straights

Dataset 5 — Validation (optional but valuable)

If you want to validate against "ground truth":

  • F1TV onboard videos showing gear/RPM/speed HUDs for cross-checking
  • Publicly released tire graphics from F1 broadcasts (Pirelli's tire stress visualization on-screen)
  • Post-race Pirelli analysis reports (qualitative, but useful sanity checks)
  • Team strategy analyses (e.g., The Race, Motorsport.com pit stop analyses) as qualitative checks

Part IV — Sequential ML calibration pipeline

Don't try to fit everything at once. Calibrate in stages, using the fact that some parameters affect independent observables.

1 Stage 1 — Aero calibration ($C_L A$, $C_D A$, $\xi$)

Observables: Maximum lateral g-force in fast corners (aero-limited), maximum speed on long straights (drag-limited), car pitch/rake behavior (aero balance).

Method:

  • For each known fast corner (e.g., Copse at Silverstone, T1 at Monza), observe the max lateral g
  • Solve: $g_{lat,max} = \mu_{grip} \cdot (g + F_{aero}/M) / g$
  • With $\mu_{grip} \approx 1.8$ as a prior, fit $C_L A$ across many corners and many tracks
  • For $C_D A$: solve terminal speed on straights where $F_{drag} = F_{thrust}$
  • For $\xi$: doesn't affect g-force directly; calibrate jointly with thermal model

Expected accuracy: ±10% on $C_L A$, ±15% on $C_D A$.

2 Stage 2 — Friction baseline ($\mu_0^{fresh}$, $\bar{p}_0$, $n$)

Observables: Peak lateral g as function of speed (tests load sensitivity), peak lateral g on fresh tires in early laps.

Method:

  • Isolate laps 2–5 of each stint (tires warmed but not yet degraded) and identify the peak lateral g in each corner
  • Compute the effective friction coefficient: $\mu_{eff}(V) = M a_{lat} / (Mg + F_{aero}(V))$
  • Plot $\ln(\mu_{eff})$ vs $\ln(\bar{p})$ across speeds; slope gives $-(1-n)$
  • Intercept and a chosen reference give $\mu_0^{fresh}$ and $\bar{p}_0$

Expected accuracy: ±5% on $\mu_0^{fresh}$, ±0.05 on $n$.

3 Stage 3 — Temperature window ($T_{opt}$, $\sigma_T$) and thermal parameters

Observables: Out-lap performance (cold tires), lap-time evolution in first 5 laps of stint (warm-up), variation in peak performance across track temperatures.

Method:

  • For each stint, identify: lap 1 time (cold), best lap time in stint (optimal), and how rapidly lap time reaches optimum
  • The rate of warm-up determines $C_{tread}$ (thermal mass)
  • The temperature at which optimum occurs determines $T_{opt}$ relative to $T_{track}$
  • The width of the optimum (how sensitive performance is to track temp variations across race days) determines $\sigma_T$
  • Fit jointly via constrained optimization: the thermal ODE must reproduce the observed warm-up curves

Expected accuracy: ±10°C on $T_{opt}$, ±5°C on $\sigma_T$, ±30% on thermal capacities.

4 Stage 4 — Degradation parameters ($\beta_{therm}, k_{wear}$)

Observables: Lap-time degradation rate in middle and late stints.

Method:

  • After Stages 1–3, the model predicts $T_{tread}(t)$ and $\mu(t)$ for each stint
  • Without degradation, $\mu$ should be constant once warm — but observed lap times keep increasing
  • The excess degradation is attributed to $\mu_0(t)$ decaying via thermal aging + wear
  • Fit $\beta_{therm}$ and $k_{wear}$ by matching observed lap-time-vs-age curves across stints
  • Use a Bayesian framework (MCMC or variational inference) to quantify uncertainty

Expected accuracy: ±30% on degradation rates (the least constrained parameters).

5 Stage 5 — Cross-validation and refinement

  • Hold out 20% of races as test set
  • Predict degradation for held-out races; compare against observed
  • Compute metrics: per-lap time prediction RMSE, stint-end grip prediction error, optimal pit window prediction accuracy
  • Iterate: parameters that fail to generalize well may need to be made track-specific or compound-specific

Part V — Data preparation checklist

Before starting model construction, gather and preprocess:

  1. FastF1 installed and cached: Set up the FastF1 Python library with a local cache directory to avoid repeated downloads. Download all 2022–2025 race sessions.
  2. Per-lap metadata clean: For each lap, verify the compound, tire age, fuel estimate, weather conditions, and whether it's an in-/out-lap (excluded from degradation analysis).
  3. Outlier filtering: Remove laps affected by safety cars, virtual safety cars, yellow flags, laps behind traffic (pace affected by car ahead), and obvious lift-and-coast fuel-saving laps.
  4. Track reference lines: For each circuit, compute the average racing line from the fastest 20% of laps (pole sitters' qualifying laps, typically). Use this to compute reference $\kappa(s)$ and $s(X, Y)$ mapping.
  5. Weather normalization: Convert all weather data to consistent units. Interpolate between ~1-min samples to per-lap values.
  6. Pirelli compound mapping: Build a table: (race, SOFT, MEDIUM, HARD) → (C5, C4, C3) or similar, since the actual compound letter changes each race.
  7. Gear ratio extraction: For each team, fit the 8 gear ratios + final drive from steady-speed telemetry.
  8. Baseline linear degradation model: As a sanity check and baseline for improvement, fit simple linear regressions of lap time vs tire age per stint per compound per race. Your physics model should do meaningfully better than this.

Part VI — Software stack

  • Data acquisition: fastf1 (Python)
  • Numerical: numpy, scipy (for ODE integration), scipy.optimize (for parameter fitting)
  • Splines and smoothing: scipy.interpolate (for track curvature), scipy.signal (for Savitzky-Golay filters)
  • ML/Bayesian: pymc or numpyro (for MCMC), optionally scikit-learn (for baseline models)
  • Visualization: matplotlib, plotly, folium (for track maps)
  • Optimization framework: optuna or skopt (for Bayesian optimization of thermal parameters)

Part VII — Expected challenges and mitigations

Challenge 1: Overfitting to training races.
With ~95 parameters and ~1000 stints of training data, there's plenty of room to overfit. Mitigate with: (a) strong physical priors on each parameter, (b) regularization penalizing deviation from physically reasonable values, (c) cross-validation with held-out races.
Challenge 2: Parameter degeneracy.
Some parameter combinations produce nearly identical predictions. For example, increasing $\mu_0$ and decreasing $T_{opt}$ might give the same lap-time prediction but different physical interpretations. Mitigate by using observables that constrain different parameters: peak g in slow corners constrains $\mu_0$, warm-up rate constrains $C_{tread}$.
Challenge 3: Track-to-track generalization.
The Mercedes paper found that specialist single-track models outperform generalist ones. You may need to allow track-specific offsets on some parameters (e.g., aero $C_L A$ varies with wing package used at each track). Consider a hierarchical model: shared "global" parameters + track-specific corrections.
Challenge 4: Compound-to-compound generalization.
Different compounds have different $T_{opt}$, $\sigma_T$, $\beta_{therm}$, etc. You can't assume one parameter set fits all compounds. But fitting each compound independently with limited data per compound is noisy. Mitigate with hierarchical priors that encode known relationships (e.g., softer compounds have lower $T_{opt}$ and higher $\beta_{therm}$).
Challenge 5: Driver effects.
Different drivers have different styles — some are harder on tires than others. This introduces ~20 additional degrees of freedom if you fit per-driver. Start by either averaging across drivers on the same team (shared setup) or fitting a single "driver aggressiveness" multiplier per driver.
